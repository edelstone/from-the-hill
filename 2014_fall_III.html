Dear Friends,
 
I'd like to share with you some items of interest about Texas State that I believe underscore our rising profile and the critical role we play in contributing to the vibrancy of the San Marcos region and the state of Texas.
 
This past Wednesday, I delivered remarks to a group of business leaders at the Greater San Marcos Innovation Summit 2014. I was invited to give an update on the state of innovation at Texas State and the impact that the university has on the Central Texas region. It's a topic I enjoy talking about because it strikes at the core of what this university is doing and how we are adding value to the region. You may read my speech at this link.
 
The centerpiece of our innovative activities is our Science, Technology and Advanced Research (STAR) Park, which this month turns two years old. Currently, the research park has one building, STAR One, but five technology clients occupy space there and we are in the process of adding six new labs to accommodate future clients. STAR Park is an ideal place for technology startups, because they benefit from the expertise of our faculty and graduate students, and from working with cutting-edge companies that are developing new technologies.
 
The research park, which often is referred to as a small business incubator and accelerator, is helping these technology startups turn into thriving companies that provide jobs and resources to the region. For now, most of these startups are focused on technologies that are related to nanomaterials, which can be used to improve many of the devices we use today, such as mobile phones and medical equipment.
 
It is significant that the summit took place just two days after global Information Technology giant NEC Corporation of Japan signed an agreement with Texas State to partner with the university on an array of projects aimed at sustaining natural resources. NEC and Texas State will be collaborating on research and development activities and using technologies developed by the IT company to address infrastructure issues such as water leakage detection. It is a fitting partnership as NEC and Texas State have demonstrated a longstanding commitment to sustainability and green technologies. What's more is the tremendous boost this provides for the university to be partnering with such a high-profile corporation on projects that are so important to society. You may read more about the NEC partnership at this link, and below is a picture taken during the signing of the agreement. With me are Nobuhiro Endo, president and CEO of NEC Corporation of Japan, and Shinsuke Takahashi, president of NEC Corporation of America.
 
 
President Denise Trauth, President Nobuhiro Endo and President Shinsuke Takahashi
 
The NEC partnership was signed just one month after the Wells Fargo Foundation gave STAR Park a $100,000 gift to be used for projects also aimed at sustainability and alternative energy technologies. More details on that gift are available at this link. That followed a gift of $1.3 million worth of equipment given this summer to the university by Nitronex, a leader in the semiconductor world. The equipment is capable of producing high-tech materials by depositing extremely thin layers, essentially atom-by-atom, on semiconductor wafers. The material is then used to manufacture devices such as amplifiers for wireless communication, light-emitting diodes (LEDs), lasers, transistors, solar cells and other electronic devices. It is extraordinarily sophisticated equipment, and it was given to us because Nitronex recognized the potential Texas State has in using it to advance technology. For more on the Nitronex gift, visit this link.
 
 
President Denise Trauth and Mark Curry, President of Wells Fargo
 
Collectively, these gifts and partnerships are added evidence that the broader corporate world is recognizing Texas State's approach to innovation and its commitment to preserving our world for generations to come. I am certain there will be more partnerships of this caliber in Texas State's future, and we promise to keep you posted.
 
Sincerely,
 
Denise M. Trauth
